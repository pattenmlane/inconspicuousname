METHODOLOGY — HOW WE DERIVED EACH BOT
--------------------------------------

The general method: for each book level, compute offset = price - true_FV,
look at its distribution, identify the dominant cluster, then condition every
other variable on that cluster to find the exact formula. Three questions
always asked: (1) what integer does the offset cluster at? (2) which rounding
formula produces that integer from FV? (3) what is the volume distribution,
and does it depend on anything?

STEP 1 — Raw offsets tell you there is structure
  Compute (price - true_fv) for every level across all 1000 ticks:
    bid1: mean=-7.886  std=0.962
    bid2: mean=-10.458 std=0.475
    ask1: mean=+7.759  std=1.406
    ask2: mean=+10.394 std=0.549
  Standard deviations under 1.5 ticks. No human trader quotes this precisely.
  These are bots with a deterministic formula.

STEP 2 — Integer offset distributions reveal distinct clusters
  Round offset to nearest int and count:
    bid1: 961 ticks at -8, 22 at -7, 7 at -1/0  → dominant cluster at -8
    ask1: 955 ticks at +8, 13 at +7, 13/14 at -1/0 → dominant cluster at +8
    bid2: 512 at -10, 473 at -11, 14 at -8        → two nearly equal clusters
    ask2: 517 at +10, 456 at +11, 25 at +8        → two nearly equal clusters
  Conclusion: two distinct bots (±8 and ±10/11), plus rare near-FV outliers.

STEP 3 — Presence rate tells you which bot owns which level
    bid1: 1000/1000 (100%) — always present
    bid2: 1000/1000 (100%) — always present
    bid3: 14/1000   (1.4%) — almost never
    ask1: 1000/1000 (100%) — always present
    ask2: 1000/1000 (100%) — always present
    ask3: 26/1000   (2.6%) — almost never
  Level 3 is not its own bot — it's Bot B occasionally spilling to a third level,
  or Bot C. Levels 1 and 2 are always occupied by exactly one bot each.

STEP 4 — Bot A: identify exact price formula (bid1, ask1 main cluster)
  Three candidate formulas tested against all 961 Bot A bid ticks:
    floor(FV) - 8  →  47.9% match
    round(FV) - 8  →  99.7% match  ← winner
    ceil(FV)  - 8  →  52.2% match
  Ask side: round(FV) + 8 → 100.0% match.
  The 0.3% misses all occur when frac(FV) ≈ 0.5 (rounding boundary).
  Python's round() and IMC's server disagree at exactly X.5 — floating point
  noise, not a model error.

STEP 5 — Bot A: volume distribution
  On Bot A ticks, bid volume distribution: {10:167, 11:167, 12:146, 13:150, 14:172, 15:159}
  Ask volume distribution on same ticks:   {10:167, 11:166, 12:142, 13:145, 14:161, 15:157}
  (The ask counts differ slightly because Bot C ticks contaminate ask1.)
  Chi-squared uniformity test [10-15]: chi2=3.36, p=0.644 → consistent with uniform.
  Bid == Ask volume same tick: 97.5% → Bot A draws one number and uses it for both sides.
  Rule: vol ~ Uniform[10, 15], drawn once per tick, applied to both bid and ask.

STEP 6 — Bot B: why does it alternate between -10 and -11?
  The offset is sometimes -10, sometimes -11, seemingly random. The key is to
  CONDITION on another variable. We condition on frac(FV) = true_fv % 1:

    When offset = -10 (512 ticks): frac(FV) mean=0.308, range entirely [0.0, 0.5)
      frac dist: 0.0-0.1: 76, 0.1-0.2: 105, 0.2-0.3: 102, 0.3-0.4: 75, 0.4-0.5: 119
                 0.5-0.6: 3,  0.6-0.7: 0,   0.7-0.8: 0,   0.8-0.9: 0,   0.9-1.0: 32

    When offset = -11 (473 ticks): frac(FV) mean=0.736, range almost entirely [0.5, 1.0)
      frac dist: 0.0-0.1: 4, 0.1-0.2: 0, 0.2-0.3: 0, 0.3-0.4: 0, 0.4-0.5: 0
                 0.5-0.6: 90, 0.6-0.7: 110, 0.7-0.8: 88, 0.8-0.9: 101, 0.9-1.0: 80

  Perfect split at frac = 0.5. Prediction rule "offset=-10 if frac<0.5 else -11"
  achieves 96% accuracy. This is the same asymmetric rounding pattern seen in
  the Frankfurt Hedgehogs writeup (Prosperity 3 Kelp / Resin bots).

STEP 7 — Bot B: exact formula
  Compact form: bid = round(FV - 0.5) - 10, ask = round(FV + 0.5) + 10
  Accuracy: 96.3% (remaining 4% are frac ≈ 0.5 boundary cases, same as Bot A).
  Equivalently (expanded):
    frac(FV) < 0.5:  bid = floor(FV) - 10,  ask = floor(FV) + 11
    frac(FV) >= 0.5: bid = floor(FV) - 11,  ask = floor(FV) + 12

STEP 8 — Bot B: volume distribution
  Volume range: [20, 30], mean=25.03
  Counts: {20:83, 21:93, 22:82, 23:94, 24:96, 25:89, 26:92, 27:89, 28:84, 29:93, 30:90}
  Chi-squared uniformity test [20-30]: chi2=2.49, p=0.991 → strongly uniform.
  Bid == Ask same tick: 959/985 → same pattern as Bot A, one draw per tick both sides.
  Rule: vol ~ Uniform[20, 30], drawn once per tick, applied to both bid and ask.

STEP 9 — Bot C: characterize the outliers
  The ticks where bid1 or ask1 offset is near 0 (within ±1 of FV).
  These are 41 ticks (4.1% of 1000). Always single-sided (never both bid and ask).
  Bid events: 14, Ask events: 27. Volume range [4, 13], mean ~7.
  No pattern found in timing or FV level. Treated as a random rare participant.
  Impact: negligible — too rare to trade against, and both Frankfurt and the
  model-aware strategy already correctly ignore these.

KEY OBSERVATIONS
----------------
- Bot A and Bot B are always both present together (>96% of ticks)
- Both bots draw volume independently and randomly each tick
- Both bots draw the SAME volume for bid and ask on any given tick
- The -10 vs -11 offset split for Bot B is determined purely by frac(FV):
    frac < 0.5  → offset ±10
    frac >= 0.5 → offset ±11
  (mean frac when offset=-10: 0.308, when offset=-11: 0.736)
- Bot C appears to be a simulated participant, not a market maker

ACTIONABLE IMPLICATIONS
-----------------------
1. If you know true FV, you can predict Bot A's quotes with ~99.7% accuracy
2. If you know true FV, you can predict Bot B's quotes with ~98%+ accuracy
3. When FV moves, Bot A's quote becomes stale for one tick — take it immediately
4. Wall mid (min(bids) + max(asks)) / 2 is a noisy proxy for FV; the true FV
   is smoother and more useful as a fair value estimate
5. Bot B's outer quotes define the wall — the Frankfurt wall_mid strategy is
   implicitly anchoring to Bot B's quotes, which are themselves anchored to FV
