--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SECTION: Implied Volatility and Moneyness
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q1 (implicit): What should you use beyond raw voucher prices to judge value?
A1: Implied volatility from a pricing model (we used Black–Scholes European call, r=0,
    T = winding DTE / 365 per timestamp, underlying = VELVETFRUIT_EXTRACT mid, option =
    voucher mid). IV encodes
    the market’s implied uncertainty (expectation and risk premium), not a guaranteed
    future volatility.

Q2: Once you have volatility figures, do they move consistently across strikes and time,
    or does the picture become uneven?
A2: Mostly a coherent SMILE (higher IV in deep ITM left wing and far OTM right wing,
    lower near ATM around 5000–5500 given S~5.2–5.3k). Across the three historical
    days, median IV by strike shifts somewhat (wings a bit higher on later days in our
    sample). The picture is not perfectly smooth: quadratic detrend in log(K/S) leaves
    systematic residuals for some strikes — see iv_residuals_detrended.png and
    iv_smile_residual_summary.csv (e.g. VEV_4500 rich vs fit, VEV_4000 / VEV_5400 cheap).

Q3: How does moneyness (strike vs underlying) relate to the volatility you calculated?
A3: IV tends to rise as |log(K/S)| grows in this data: deep ITM (low K/S) and far OTM
    (high K/S) carry more IV; the ATM neighborhood is flatter / lower. Plot IV vs
    log(K/S) in iv_vs_logmoneyness_scatter.png and iv_smile_snapshots.png.

Q4: If you plot IV against moneyness, what is the market implying?
A4: A volatility smile / skewed surface: participants demand more “insurance” / lottery
    convexity away from the money. Localized kinks (residuals off a simple parabola)
    suggest RELATIVE cheap vs rich names vs that smooth benchmark — not by itself a
    full trading rule.

Q5 (quote): “Your job, before you do anything else, is to understand what structure the
    market is currently implying. Everything else follows from that.” — What structure?
A5: Cross-sectionally: smile-shaped IV in log-moneyness with ATM valley and wing peaks.
    Intra-surface: persistent misalignment pockets (see residual table). Temporally:
    deviations fluctuate (price_deviation timeseries plots); lag-1 autocorrelation of
    (mid − theoretical price using iv_fit) is in price_deviation_lag1_autocorr.csv /
    price_deviation_lag1_autocorr_bars.png — use that to judge short-horizon persistence
    vs mean reversion for sizing/scalping hypotheses.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SECTION: Positioning With IV and Moneyness
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q6: Does the picture hold together, or do certain vouchers sit where they should not?
A6: Globally it holds (one smile). Locally VEV_4000, VEV_4500, VEV_5400, VEV_6000 show
    the largest systematic departures from a single quadratic in log(K/S) across days
    in our subsampled IV panel — “odd ones out” for further price-space and execution
    work.

Q7: Are deviations over- or underestimating uncertainty vs neighbors?
A7: Positive IV residual ~ higher IV than neighbors (e.g. VEV_4500, VEV_6000 in places).
    Negative residual ~ lower IV than the fitted smile (e.g. VEV_4000, VEV_5400).
    Interpret as RELATIVE richness/cheapness to the chosen smile model, not ground truth.

Q8: Does deviation automatically mean opportunity?
A8: No. It means misalignment vs the model curve. Whether to trade, and which side,
    needs edge after spreads, limits, inventory, and how fast mispricing mean-reverts.

Q9: Who decides buy, sell, or stay put?
A9: The trader. Structure supplies diagnostics; it does not replace judgment.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SECTION: Let’s Talk Volume
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q10: After spotting mispricing, how much should you commit?
A10: Scale with (i) magnitude of deviation, (ii) confidence in the read, (iii) how wrong
     you can be without ruin. Position limits (300 per voucher in the spec) cap size.

Q11: Should a larger deviation imply a larger position?
A11: In principle a stronger signal can justify more risk budget; in practice coupling
     should be sub-linear (model error, fat tails on wings, liquidity).

Q12: What is the downside of larger volume?
A12: Returns and losses both scale; wrong vol read + adverse path = larger drawdown.

Q13: Can a well-reasoned volatility interpretation still be wrong?
A13: Yes — surfaces can stay dislocated or move against you.

Q14: “How confident are you in your reading? And how much damage can your strategy absorb?”
A14: Answer with explicit risk limits, stress on underlying + options book, and worst-case
     inventory paths — not with narrative conviction alone.

Q15: “Conviction is not certainty.” What does that imply for sizing?
A15: Size to survive being wrong; treat high conviction as still probabilistic.

Q16: Should volume reflect conviction or hope?
A16: Conviction backed by evidence (structure + execution + backtest), not hope.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
EMPIRICAL SNAPSHOT (this folder’s subsampled historical run)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Price deviation = voucher mid minus Black–Scholes price using smile-fitted IV (iv_fit)
from a quadratic in log(K/S) at each timestamp. Lag-1 autocorrelation of that series
per (day, voucher) is in price_deviation_lag1_autocorr.csv.

Observation: on this grid (every 20th timestep + endpoints), lag-1 autocorrelations
are mostly POSITIVE for many strikes (not strongly negative mean-reversion). That
does not kill scalping hypotheses (different horizons, full timestep grid, or raw
mid changes may differ), but it DOES warn against assuming Frankfurt-style negative
lag-1 in price deviations without re-checking on your exact bar frequency and fair-vol
definition. Near-ATM names (e.g. VEV_5000) show moderate positive persistence in this
sample (e.g. day 2 lag1 ~ +0.17 for VEV_5000).

Use price_deviation_lag1_autocorr_bars.png and the CSV to compare strikes and days.
